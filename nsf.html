Government Advancement of Technology through the National Science Foundation

    Governmentally funded academic venues and science programs have shaped computer science progress through the funding of research projects and work on large-scale endeavors. Specifically, the National Science Foundation had a large impact on technological advancement, including on the creation of the Internet. Computers have shaped the function of the National Science Foundation and affected the course of government involvement in the sciences, and the National Science Foundation has also changed the course of technological innovation. The key role of the National Science Foundation (NSF) in technology is particularly notable in the generation of the World Wide Web, which demonstrates the impact of governmental funding on technology. The present day involvement of governmentally-funded science programs on computer science research and technology is still very much active, as visible through domain registries and research funded by the NSF. The social context revolves around governmentally- funded science institutions, while the computing context focuses on the technology behind networking/supercomputers and the World Wide Web.

     Computers shaped the function of the National Science Foundation because the NSF was specifically created to further the sciences, including scientific progress. One of the goals of the NSF is to promote scientific progress, which is accomplished through research, the creation of scientific and engineering programs, education programs for all levels of science and engineering, and dispersal of information for policy formulation ("National Science Foundation.”) Upon the exploration of supercomputer centers and networks, the NSF devoted resources to computer science and technological advancement.

     The National Science Foundation changed the course of technological innovation especially in its work on the World Wide Web. The Internet arose out of a number of governmentally funded computer networking services.      The creation of the NSF’s NSFNET was key component of the supercomputer initiative. The NSFNET was a general high-sped network which connected existing networks, including DARPA’s internet, the ARPANET(“The Internet - The Launch of NSFNET”). With the success of the National Science Foundation Network (NSFNET) and expansion of capabilities one of the key features that surfaced was domain name registration. According to Karen Sandberg, the Administrative Officer of the National Science Foundation, the NSF paid for a domain name registry and it was free for public use. The number of domain registries gradually grew to nearly thousands of registrations a month. As the NSFNET continued to grow, the idea of browsers surfaced, and the NSF was supporting the University of Illinois, including Marc Andreessen, who developed Mosaic, the first successful browser. Karen Sandberg reflects on the early days of the NSFNET and its impact on the creation of browsers: “The Internet allowed so much information to be available and shared among researchers and other organizations, but it was really difficult to find the information that people were looking for. And so that’s what browsers were intended for” (“NSF and the Birth of the Internet,” 2007). The NSFNET also sparked the creation of Internet browsers out of the necessity to easily organize and categorize information.

     As the Web grew and expanded past the academic arena, the National Science Foundation supported projects to simplify and clarify the Web. (“NSF and the Birth of the Internet,” 2007). Via NSFNET, the NSF allowed for thousands of thousands of new users to use the Internet every month. Then, private companies did not require special access to link into the Web, allowing it to grow quickly without issue. This NSF-built structure then allowed other countries to link into the Internet in addition to private businesses (“NSF and the Birth of the Internet,” 2007).

     On the success of NSFNET, George O. Strawn, the Chief Information Officer of the NSF, states: “There’s no way that the government could have or would have wanted to [build the Internet] themselves when it hit an explosive growth phase and I think the [...] entrepreneurs and the companies that provided backbone services all of a sudden found that they had [...] a much bigger job on their hands than they realized to be.” The internet revolution furthered the success of the telegraph from the previous century, and under the NSFNET, the government created a 24-￼node network and a 2,000 node network. Strawn continues: this network “created [...] enough impetus that the private sector said this is ready [for] commercial use that all of a sudden we had something that [turned] out to be even bigger than the telegraph network of the 19th Century had been.” As Strawn illuminates, the NSF played the key role in bringing the Internet to the private sector, because the initial development of the Internet would have been two hefty a task for individual businesses to take on. Thus, a public-private partnership was built on behalf of technological development (“NSF and the Birth of the Internet,” 2007).

     The present day involvement of the NSF on computer science research and technology revolves around financial support of research projects. After NSFNET, the NSF continues to impact the Internet and networking through its support of research projects on networking tool development, education use of the Web, and network-based applications ("A Brief History of NSF and the Internet,” 2003).

     One of these university research project financed by the National Science Foundation was a language learning application called Duolingo. Doulingo’s exploratory research was funded by a $120,000 a year, 5-year grant from the NSF. Just two years later, the company had a working prototype and enough progress to build a company and find private investors. Duolingo recently earned $15 million of venture capital from private investors to continue to fund the work in Natural Language Processing. Governmental support plays a key role in the incubation of new ideas that are then used and funded by the private sector.      Though the National Science Foundation’s involvement in technology began in the 1990s through work on the World Wide Web, the NSF continues to support technological feats in the modern day by funding computer science research. The National Science Foundation contributes to research, such as the building of the Internet, that the government itself wouldn’t have been able to support given the rapid growth of the industry. Government funding of science programs shapes computer science by structuring a large-scale organization specifically intended to further computer science research. Because the NSF is an organized foundation with expansive resources, it is more equipped to handle computer science research than either individual entrepreneurs or the government itself.
